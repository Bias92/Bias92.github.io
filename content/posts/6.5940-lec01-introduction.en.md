## The Growth of DNNs and the Need for Efficiency

In the ImageNet ILSVRC competition, after AlexNet drove the Top-5 Error Rate down to 16.4% in 2012, the field progressed through GoogLeNet (6.7%) → ResNet (3.6%) → SENet (2.3%), surpassing the Human Error Rate (5.1%) already from 2015. Model accuracy went up, but so did size and compute, explosively, and that is the fundamental reason Efficient Deep Learning is needed.

The rate at which model size grows overwhelmingly outpaces the rate at which GPU memory grows. Transformer (0.05B, 2017) → GPT-2 (1.5B) → GPT-3 (175B) → MT-NLG (530B, 2022). Roughly a 10,000x increase in 5 years. Over the same period GPU memory only grew about 5x, from TPUv2 16GB → A100 80GB. This gap means the model no longer fits on a single GPU, which is why distributed techniques such as tensor parallelism are needed.

ChatGPT's "at capacity" message and its limit of 50 messages per 3 hours are real-world examples of just how severe the cost of LLM serving is.

## Redundancy: The Core Premise

Every technique in Efficient ML starts from a single observation: DNNs contain a great deal of redundancy. A substantial fraction of a neural network's weights either do not meaningfully contribute to the final output, or perform a redundant, overlapping role.

The existence of this redundancy is the precondition for every compression technique:
- Why Pruning works: performance is maintained even after removing unnecessary weights
- Why Quantization works: important information is preserved even when precision is lowered
- Why Distillation works: only the core knowledge needs to fit into a small model
- Why large models are robust to quantization: they have more redundancy, so the rest can compensate for the precision loss of some weights

## Model Compression

Model Compression is the umbrella category, and its sub-techniques are as follows:

- Pruning: remove unimportant weights, neurons, heads, tokens
- Quantization: lower precision through FP32 → FP16 → INT8 → INT4 and so on
- Knowledge Distillation: a large model (teacher) teaches a small model (student)
- Low-rank Factorization: decompose a weight matrix into a product of low-rank matrices
- Architecture Design / NAS: design an efficient structure in the first place

The key point is that you do not use just one technique: combining them multiplies the effect. In the Lite Transformer example, applying architecture (Lite Transformer) + quantization + pruning in sequence achieved 176MB → 9.7MB (an 18.2x reduction), with BLEU 39.9 → 39.5 (almost no difference).

## Integer vs Floating Point: INT8/INT4 vs FP32/FP16

To understand Quantization, you need to understand the difference in data representations.

Floating Point: FP32, FP16, BF16, and so on. Capable of representing decimals with a wide dynamic range. It can represent fine-grained values like 0.3752 and -1.2841.

Integer: INT8 (-128 to 127), INT4 (-8 to 7), and so on. It can only represent a fixed integer range, with no decimals.

What Quantization does is map FP16's continuous weight distribution onto INT8/INT4's discrete values. It multiplies by a scale factor to convert into integers.

Why use integers: FP16 is 2 bytes per weight, INT8 is 1 byte, INT4 is 0.5 byte. Integer arithmetic is faster in hardware than floating point and consumes less power. The catch is a trade-off with accuracy.

The actual usage pattern is to mix them:
- Training: FP32 or BF16 (accuracy first)
- Inference (server): FP16 or W8A8 (balance)
- Inference (edge): W4A16 or W4A8 (memory constrained)
- Attention computation: keep FP16 (softmax precision issue)

In the latest NVIDIA Blackwell (B100/B200), hardware support was added all the way down to FP4 (NVFP4). Being 4-bit while retaining floating point semantics, it has a wider dynamic range than INT4 and shows results with under 1% accuracy drop relative to FP8.

## Core Quantization Techniques

### RTN (Round-To-Nearest)
The simplest quantization. It rounds each weight to the nearest integer. It is fine for large models (70B+) or higher bit widths (INT8), but at INT4 + a small model (7B) important information is lost. The example of looking at the Mona Lisa and failing to answer "Leonardo da Vinci" illustrates this well.

### AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantization)
A technique from Song Han's lab. Based on the observation that "not all weights are equally important," it analyzes the activation distribution and quantizes while protecting the important weight channels. It has effectively become the standard for weight-only quantization (W4A16). At the same INT4, its quality gap over RTN is large.

### SmoothQuant
A technique that solves the activation outlier problem. Originally, activations are hard to quantize because outliers spike out sharply, while weights are easy to quantize because their distribution is uniform. Through the mathematical transformation `Y = XW = (X/s)(sW)`, SmoothQuant migrates the quantization difficulty from the activation side to the weight side. As a result, both become easy to quantize (W8A8).

### TinyChatEngine
A lightweight LLM inference engine written from scratch in C/C++ at Han Lab. It serves AWQ-quantized models on edge devices. There is a demo running LLaMA-2-7B W4A16 at 30 tokens/s on a Jetson Orin.

## Sparsity and Pruning

Sparsity: a state in which most values in a matrix are 0. Since multiplication by 0 always yields 0, the computation itself can be skipped, which reduces the amount of compute.

Pruning → Sparsity: Pruning is the process of setting unimportant weights to 0, which makes the model sparse.

Structured Sparsity (2:4): introduced on the NVIDIA A100. The hardware Tensor Core directly recognizes a pattern where 2 of every 4 values must be 0, and skips computation for the values that are 0. In theory, a 2x speedup.

This is why, in the GPU performance chart seen earlier, the A100 jumped so sharply over the Q8000: INT8 Tensor Cores (261 TOPS) → with Structured Sparsity applied (1248 TOPS).

## SpAtten: Token/Head-level Dynamic Pruning

SpAtten is a technique that prunes unimportant tokens and heads at runtime during attention computation.

When performing sentiment classification on the sentence "As a visual treat, the film is almost perfect":
- Layer 1: 11 tokens, 12 heads → 100% compute
- Layer 2: 5 tokens, 10 heads → 38%
- Layer 3: 2 tokens, 8 heads → 12%
- Final: successfully classified as Positive using just "film perfect"

Accumulating attention probability vertically gives an importance score for each token. Function words like "I", "a", "the" have low cumulative importance, while meaningful tokens like "video", "game", "fun" are high. The low tokens are dropped at the next layer (cascade pruning).

The key point is that this is not weight pruning but token-level + head-level dynamic pruning. Because which tokens get dropped changes with each input, it is more flexible than static pruning. If FlashAttention captures the memory efficiency of attention, SpAtten is a complementary approach that reduces the amount of attention compute itself.

## On-Device / Edge AI

When data cannot be sent to the cloud (privacy, offline environments, personalization), inference and training must be performed directly on the edge device.

The problem is that training consumes far more memory than inference:
- Inference: a single forward pass and you are done
- Training: forward → store all activations → compute gradients via backward → update optimizer state

### MCUNet V3: Making Training Possible on a 256KB MCU

The MCUNet V3 paper reports a 2300x reduction by cutting training memory from a 303MB PyTorch baseline down to 141KB. Compared with the 652MB TensorFlow (cloud) baseline, the reduction is about 4600x. The result of applying three techniques in sequence:

1. Quantization-Aware Scaling: correct the gradient by the squared inverse of the weight's scale. 5.7MB → 2.9MB (2.0x)
2. Sparse Layer Update: selectively perform backward only on the important layers. 2.9MB → 335KB (8.8x)
3. Sparse Tensor Update: update only the important parts even within a tensor. 335KB → 141KB (2.4x)

## VLM and VILA

- ViT (Vision Transformer): a vision encoder that processes images only. A structure that splits an image into patches and feeds them into a Transformer.
- VLM (Vision Language Model): ViT + LLM. A model that understands both images and text. LLaVA, GPT-4V, Claude, and so on fall into this category.
- LLaVA: a structure of Vision Encoder (CLIP ViT) + Projection Layer + LLM (LLaMA).
- VILA: a VLM that improves on LLaVA, from the NVIDIA + MIT (Song Han) lab. It can be quantized with AWQ on Jetson for edge deployment.

For reference, VLM (image → text, understanding) and Stable Diffusion (text → image, generation) run in opposite directions. What they have in common is roughly that they both use a vision-language alignment model such as CLIP.

## Hardware Trend: Memory Wall and Power Efficiency

### The End of Dennard Scaling and Dedicated Compute Units

In 42 years of microprocessor data, transistor count keeps increasing, but clock frequency and single-thread performance have stagnated since around 2005 (the end of Dennard Scaling). The solution was a shift from adding general-purpose cores to dedicated compute units (Tensor Core and the like).

Single-Chip Inference Performance improved 317x in 8 years by lowering precision (FP32 → FP16 → INT8) and supporting sparsity at the hardware level (K20X 3.94 TOPS → A100 1248 TOPS).

### Memory Wall

Compute performance (TOPS) jumps 2 to 3x per generation, but HBM bandwidth increases at about a 2x level. As this gap accumulates, the memory wall problem worsens. Compute is fast, but the speed of fetching data from memory becomes the bottleneck.

This is the fundamental reason FlashAttention exists: even if you increase the amount of compute (recomputation), reducing memory access (HBM read/write) is faster overall.

### Cloud vs Edge Hardware

| | Performance | Power | TOPS/W (power efficiency) |
|---|---|---|---|
| NVIDIA B100 | 1,750 TOPS | 700W | 2.5 |
| Qualcomm S8G1 | 52 TOPS | 10W | 5.2 |
| Apple ANE (A17) | 35 TOPS | 8W | 4.4 |

Edge chips have lower absolute performance, but their power efficiency (TOPS/W) is actually higher. Because of battery, heat, and size constraints, they are designed to squeeze out as much compute as possible per watt.

This is why efficient ML matters more on the edge. If your power budget is only 10W, you have to reduce compute in software through quantization/pruning.
